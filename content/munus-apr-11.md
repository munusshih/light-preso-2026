---
title: Why we started this group?
date: April 11, 2026
author: Munus Shih
---

I’ve been missing how much I used to learn from taking classes. As a full-time teacher now, I often have to be the “lecturer,” even though I truly believe in critical pedagogy, where everyone has something to share. There’s a responsibility to hold the room in formal educational institutions, and it’s hard for me to create a space where I can just actively listen, especially when I’m also the one grading. I miss the kind of casual space where we can just sit and learn new things together.

Over the past year, AI has really taken off. It’s kind of funny because I actually studied and majored in AI & Deep Learning back in undergrad. At the time, I learned about the three waves of AI and thought deep learning was just the end of a pretty boring bubble cycle. It didn’t feel like something that would fundamentally shift everything. I didn’t really think it would become this big. I was very wrong, obviously. When ChatGPT first came out, I thought it would mostly impact quasi-developers like myself. It felt like a good partner in crime that I could rely on when I want to slack off at work for things like writing PHP or handling backend queries that I didn’t really want to deal with. It stayed in that small, practical zone for me for a really long time. It was the equivalent of Grammarly and a search engine hybrid. I didn’t realize it would become something that feels this dangerous now.

I’ve spent almost a full year listening to people talk about AI. The news has been terrible. It’s hard to ignore how often it’s tied to military use, to surveillance, to harm. The ways it can be used for killing civilians, and how close it feels that the code we write or help write could actually harm people, is terrifying. That distance between weapons of mass destruction and creative coding partner feels like it’s collapsing. I also feel like people around me, and myself sometimes, operate from a place of fear when it comes to AI. Have you been bombarded with ads on how grok can be a friend to you in need, or how figma make can build that app you've always wanted, or how claude can get you the job you need without actually learning how to code, even though it's not true. They show up in urgency, in anxiety, amplifying a narrative that we’re always behind. I hate these. I hate the way they make me feel. I hate the way they make people around me feel. I hate the way they make us feel like we have to be in a constant state of panic and anxiety.

I remember those moments in school where we talked about building alternative open source futures. Though some conversations were a little naive, they made me feel empowered. Code used to feel like a place where I had agency, where I could fork alternative things, propose alternatives, and imagine different systems. It felt like a tool for possibility, not just optimization. How can we take that back?

Inspired by the idea of “trade school” and the need for peer-learning spaces around AI, I frame this learning group as a potluck. A casual working group where we each bring a dish, where we share a meal, collectively learn, unlearn, teach, design, make art, and share ideas about how we want to navigate all of this. All of us together are designers, artists, creative technologists, writers, researchers, and teachers. We’re coming in with different experiences, different concerns, different skills. It feels exciting to bring everyone together and explore what it means to use “AI,” with a much more expanded definition, in our daily lives and practices.

This repository will serve as a shared writing space for the group. Everything is open source. We will use markdown files to create content, which renders directly in the browser without any build step. It’s a way to keep things light, especially when AI keeps pushing toward more complex solutions.

Each of us comes with different experiences, and I really believe everyone has something meaningful to share. It could feel like a study group, sometimes even like a support group. Some days might be more technical, some more reflective. I don’t want to limit what this can be, which is why I keep thinking of it as a trade school.

The idea that people, just by being together, can make something better feels very not AI. Though we only have 2 and a half beautiful hours to share, I want it to be slower, more intentional, and relational.

“The future is not something we enter. The future is something we create.” I want to take that seriously. I want to create a future where we can learn and support each other in this moment of change.
