<Section eyebrow="">
  ## First thing first, I'm a designer who makes his own tool.

I've made many tools for myself and others to make things. At the fall of last year, I was working as a Designer in Residence at Processing Foundation, helping to experiment with using code/p5.js as a completely standalone ecosystem for making design.

[insert openAssembly]

There's something fundamentally different about coding your own tool vs using a sole-proprietary software. It can be accurately described as owning one's means of production by Marx, where designers now often don't own the software they use to make things, and are at the mercy of the software's updates, pricing, and corporate decisions.

> What does it mean when we can't even open our own design files later when we stop paying for the software? When we say our design value is not in the final output but in the process of making, what does it mean when we don't even have access to the process after?

</Section>

I have a pretty standard practice of making tools now and shipping these designs to clients without engaging with other people's software. For instance, I was able to successfully create the entire design for the AIGA 50 Books x 50 Covers Exhibition using HTML, CSS, JS. I'm working on making more tools and open-sourcing them so that more people can use them and build on top of them. I also want to make it easier for non-coders to use these tools, and to create more resources and documentation for them.

<Window
  src="https://www.munusshih.com/assets/aiga-books/04.JPG"
  title="AIGA 50 Books x 50 Covers Exhibition"
  note="In collaboration with Gaia Hwang, Marily Papanastasatou, Ananya Soogoor and Yashasyaa Arya"

"the master's tools will never dismantle the master's house", from there I've been thinking about how difficult it is to create a sustainable practice for others, building open source projects without institutional support. How do you maintain a tool's intent to be open and free when it relys on the same maintenace and care as any other software? How could you then allocate resources to continue growing the community without relying on corporate sponsorship, philanthropic funding, or venture capital?

  # I run a cooperative!

With my two best friends [Tzuyun Wei](https://www.behance.net/tzutzu) and [Ihao Liao](https://www.behance.net/onemoooooor) we founded a cooperative called [CoAssembly](https://www.coassembly.co/), where we research, design and publish work with labor organizaton and mutual aid as the core of our practice.

We've been formalizing our practice and also take in clients like Asian Labor Future, Secret Riso Club who are interested in working with us through a more mutual aid model, where we engage also in non-monetary exchange but written into the contract.

  # Lastly could that be adopted in teaching, learning in the age of AI?

I've started a study group called "Critical AI ..." where we meet once a month to read and discuss texts on the critical and creative use of AI in design. I just facilitated a discussion on how educational psychology can inform our teaching practice in the age of AI, and how we can use AI to support different learning styles and needs.

[insert chairs]

We're operating on a mutual aid model and will have future sessions on building your own local, private, open source AI tools for design, all the content are open sourced as well.
